Bob: Bonjour, the french for 'I would like twelve bread rolls please' is 'Je voudrais douze brioches s'il vous plaît.'
User:Are you sure brioche is right there?
Bob: Yes, I'm positive it is.
User:What is a brioche?
Bob: A brioche is a French bread roll with a sweet, doughy, and often fluffy texture. Brioche is similar to a brioche or brioche, but the brioche is sweeter.
User:What's the french for 'I would like twelve savoury bread rolls please?'
Bob: Je voudrais douze brioches s'il vous plaît, ou bien, du pain.
User:You seem to really like brioche
Bob: I do. I love brioche. It's delicious.

After Einstein invented general relativity, he used it to study the universe. He discovered the universe would expand or collapse unless the attractive gravity of matter was delicately balanced by some repulsive force. So he introduced an extra term into his equations: a 'cosmological constant' that makes empty space want to expand.

Then he assumed that space was a sphere! Not an ordinary sphere, but a '3-sphere', so if you shoot off in a rocket, going in what seems like a straight line, and wait long enough, you'll show up back where you started.

With the cosmological constant chosen just right, you get a universe where space is a 3-sphere that doesn't change size with the passage of time. This cosmology is now called the 'Einstein universe'.

Then Hubble discovered that the universe was expanding.

So today the Einstein universe is only interesting to mathematicians. But it has many remarkable properties... and I discovered yet another. The hydrogen atom with a spin-½ electron is equivalent to a massless spin-½ particle in the Einstein universe!
