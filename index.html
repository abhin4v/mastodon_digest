Imagine the following music streaming scenario:

  1. Alice listens to 50 Weyes Blood songs in a month (suppose that she only listens to that artist in the whole month)
  2. Bob listens to 200 Taylor Swift songs in a month (suppose that he only listens to that artist in the whole month)
  3. Both pay $10/month for a Spotify subscription
  4. Spotify keeps a 30% share of the subscription revenue and forwards the remaining 70% to the artist

Here’s two different ways to implement point 4.

1. Proportionally (out of each user’s listening data)

  1. Weyes Blood gets 100% 70% $10 = $7 from Alice
  2. Taylor Swift gets 100% 70% $10 = $7 from Bob
  3. Spotify gets $6 from operating revenue commissions

2. Proportionally (out of a shared pool)

  1. All the subscription revenue gets funnelled to a shared pool
  2. The revenue in the pool gets split across artists proportionally to their share of the total number of streams
  3. Spotify still gets its $3/month/user commission revenue
  4. Taylor Swift currently accounts for at least 80% of all the songs streamed on Spotify - a back-of-the-napkin estimate based on Spotify’s most streamed artists stats from 2024 and the total number of streamed hours on the platform
  5. This means that, no matter what you listen, Taylor Swift will always grab 80% of the revenue

Many would be surprised to know that Spotify (and most of the other music streaming platforms) operate with model 2 rather than model 1.

Since the peak in listening statistics is extremely sharp and the tail is extremely long (because these platforms tend to push in a very limited set of curated playlists only a small subset of content that is already extremely popular, which reinforces feedback loops and discourages discovery), we have a situation where ~95% of all the Spotify streams is concentrated in the top 50 (and probably even top 20) artists.

These artists grab 95% of the revenue, and the remaining millions of artists only get breadcrumbs - no matter how hard they try.

To provide a concrete example: an indie artist (but relatively popular in her niche) like Weyes Blood has ~1.7M million monthly listeners.

A respectable number at first sight. Let’s assume that those folks listen on average to 5 of her songs per month, and her songs are ~4:30 long on average. That’s about 7.7M hours of streamed content per year. That looks even more respectable.

Suppose that her songs make up 4-5% of the songs streamed by the average listener.

And we also know that in 2024 Spotify paid $10B to artists. Which means that Taylor Swift alone grabbed $8B/year just from Spotify streams.

With this data, under model 1, an artist like Weyes Blood would get:

n_listeners = 1.7e6

The Hayashi Track and the Forbidden Zone

When a star first forms, it is powered not by nuclear fusion but simply by gravity. It shrinks, which causes a release of gravitational energy. This tends to heat it, which slows its shrinking. But it releases energy in the form of light, which tends to cool it down. Then it can keep shrinking. So you need math to figure out exactly what happens.

In 1961, Chushiro Hayashi figured it out. Stars between one tenth and twice the mass of the Sun begin life by shrinking while staying at the same temperature! As a result their overall luminosity goes down. This is called the 'Hayashi track', and you can see it happening in the vertical blue lines here.

Later, the star's surface cools down - but for some reason the star expands enough to get brighter! Then it's on the 'Henyey track', which I don't understand.

This change happens sooner for more massive stars. You see it happening when the blue lines bend to the right and go back up.

The red lines say how old a star is. You can see that our Sun followed the Hayashi track for about 10 million years. In the process, its luminosity decreased by a factor of 10.

The region to the right of the blue lines is called the 'forbidden zone'. It's impossible for stars to stay here for long, because they're highly convective: hot gas from below rises to the surface, quickly cooling these stars. When stars are first born they start out here - but they very quickly move to the Hayashi track.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayashi_track

Fixed Output Derivations
#nixos

Normally, Nix doesn’t allow derivations (= build steps) to access the network2.

If you want network access from a derivation to download some sources, Nix requires you to set clear expectations: an output hash. A derivation with such a predetermined output hash is called “Fixed Output Derivation” or, abbreviated, “FOD”.

This prevents you from silently introducing randomness into your build. If you interact with the network, you interact with the messy world beyond Nix’s control. But, at least, Nix can shout and abort the build, when your derivation does not produce the expected output.
Enter caching (substitution)
Nix will helpfully cache the result by the name of the derivation and the output hash.3 You have given Nix a promise: Trust me, whatever command I specify, it will result in the given hash. If Nix can ascertain the right result, why do all the hard work?

So what's it like to use Linux as my main desktop OS for almost half a year now?

It's like I used to live in a fancy apartment with fine furniture and abstract art on the walls, everything "smart" and fully automated, except it wasn't mine. It belonged to an abusive landlord, who liked to come and go as they pleased, changing and rearranging whatever they wanted, sometimes telling me no, I can't do this or that for whatever reason. Every few months they'd increase the rent and install more cameras to make sure I didn't do anything they didn't like.

It has cool gadgets and contraptions too, but some of them are unfinished, in some places you can see the wiring where the plaster fell of the walls, and there's one room with a hole in the floor but we just put a wooden board over it and it's fine - we'll fix it eventually.
