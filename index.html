#Etymology
Girls employed to dance in clubs, often in short skirts & high boots, were gogo dancers.

They were named for a Hollywood club (founded 1964) called Whisky a Go Go.

Which was named after a bar in the French town of Juan-les-Pins, Whisky à GoGo.

The club got its name from a film called Whisky à Gogo.

Whisky à GoGo was the French translation of Whisky Galore, a Scottish comedy film. (Gogo means "galore.")

So you see, if you explore anything deeply enough it leads back to Scotland!

One of the other changes I'm making in this iteration is getting rid of the brass threaded inserts. Really, if those hold, plastic threads will hold too. I've found that I can print working threads with my Qidi X-Max 3, and unless I clean them out with a tap or print them with loose tolerance, they are kind of like nyloc nuts. They work both for vertical and horizontal holes, too!

This broke when bed adhesion failure on an overnight print led to a nearly one-pound ball of ABS enveloping the ducts and putting so much pressure on the hot end that it broke the bracket. At that point, it really doesn't matter whether it's held on with threads into plastic or brass.

I think that the main reason to use the inserts is not for strength. It's for when you expect to disassemble and reassemble something often. These will be rarely disassembled, so there's not much point in the extra work of melting inserts into the plastic.

The attached partially-transparent view shows some of the modeled threads.

And again, FreeCAD 1.0 came through nicely. Working on the bracket the orbiter is mounted on top of and which holds the heat sink, I moved all the holes to the end of the body so that modeling threads would be at the end of the tree, and deleted all the intermediate pockets for brass inserts, and nothing broke. This is awesome!
